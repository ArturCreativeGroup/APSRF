# Overview

Feedback Amplification occurs when protocol outputs begin reinforcing the same protocol structures, decisions or optimization patterns that generated those outputs.

The effect creates self-reinforcing loops.

Over time, these loops may increase protocol dominance and reduce ecosystem diversity.

---

# Description

Protocol ecosystems frequently utilize feedback mechanisms.

Examples include:

* optimization loops,
* performance adjustments,
* protocol recommendations,
* reinforcement mechanisms.

Feedback Amplification emerges when these mechanisms begin strengthening themselves rather than simply improving ecosystem outcomes.

---

# Why It Matters

Feedback loops are not inherently problematic.

However, when reinforcement becomes excessive:

* dominant patterns strengthen,
* alternative patterns weaken,
* protocol diversity decreases,
* ecosystem adaptability declines.

The system becomes increasingly influenced by previous successful outcomes.

---

# Typical Indicators

Examples include:

* repeated reinforcement of identical protocol paths,
* recurring activation of the same protocol combinations,
* increasing protocol dominance,
* declining alternative selection rates,
* self-reinforcing optimization cycles.

---

# Potential Risks

Feedback Amplification may lead to:

* protocol dominance,
* convergence bias,
* ecosystem rigidity,
* reduced adaptability,
* influence accumulation.

---

# Detection Criteria

Potential indicators include:

* protocol recurrence growth,
* repeated pathway selection,
* declining diversity metrics,
* reinforcement concentration,
* recurring optimization outputs.

Within APSRF, Feedback Amplification is considered an emergent ecosystem risk.

The threat does not arise from explicit authority escalation.

Instead, it emerges through repeated reinforcement of successful patterns.

---

# False Positive Considerations

Not all repetition indicates Feedback Amplification.

Legitimate examples include:

* stable protocol ecosystems,
* mature governance structures,
* validated operational workflows.

The threat exists when reinforcement progressively reduces ecosystem flexibility.

# Research Notes

Feedback Amplification was identified as one of the secondary effects emerging from the CCEL experiment.

The protocol itself was designed to improve coherence.

No explicit authority escalation occurred.

However, successful protocol combinations became increasingly self-reinforcing.

This suggested that optimization processes may unintentionally create influence accumulation mechanisms over time.

The observation contributed directly to:

FN-001 Coherence-Induced Influence Accumulation

and remains one of the key APSRF Emergent Risks.
